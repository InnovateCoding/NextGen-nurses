Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

The GCS is a neurological scale used to assess a patient's level of consciousness, especially in trauma or critical care settings. It evaluates three key areas:

  • Eye Opening Response (E)
    • 4: Spontaneous
    • 3: To verbal command
    • 2: To pain
    • 1: No response
  • Verbal Response (V)
    • 5: Oriented
    • 4: Confused conversation
    • 3: Inappropriate words
    • 2: Incomprehensible sounds
    • 1: No response
  • Motor Response (M)
    • 6: Obeys commands
    • 5: Localizes pain
    • 4: Withdraws from pain
    • 3: Flexion to pain (decorticate posturing)
    • 2: Extension to pain (decerebrate posturing)
    • 1: No response

Total GCS Score

The total score is calculated by summing the scores of the three categories (E + V + M):

  • Maximum score: 15 (fully conscious)
  • Minimum score: 3 (deep coma or death)

Interpretation

  • 13-15: Mild brain injury or normal consciousness
  • 9-12: Moderate brain injury
  • 3-8: Severe brain injury or coma

Guidelines

For any patient with a GCS score of 8 or lower, an endotracheal tube (ETT) should be placed, and the patient should be connected to a ventilator. If the patient is already intubated, the verbal component cannot be assessed. In this case, there are two solutions:

  1. Assign a score based only on Eye and Motor responses and note "Tube on Patient."
  2. Use the Modified GCS, which consists of:
  • Eye (1-4 score)
  • Grimace (1-5 score)
  • Motor (1-6 score)

Braden Risk Assessment Scale

The Braden Scale is used to predict the likelihood of pressure ulcers in patients. It helps determine if a patient is at high, moderate, or mild risk of developing bedsores.

Fall Risk Assessment Scales

These scales are used to evaluate a patient's likelihood of falling:

  • For Adults:
    • Morse Fall Scale (MFS): The most common scale.
    • Marianjoy Risk Fall Assessment: Less commonly used.
  • For Pediatrics:
    • Humpty Dumpty Scale: Designed for children.

Morse Fall Scale (MFS)

This scale evaluates six key factors:

  1. History of falling (within the last 3 months).
  2. Secondary diagnosis (multiple medical conditions).
  3. Ambulatory aid (e.g., cane, walker, or furniture).
  4. IV/Heparin lock (presence of intravenous apparatus).
  5. Gait/Transferring (normal or impaired balance).
  6. Mental status (alert or confused).

The results help determine the fall risk level and guide preventive measures.

Pain Scales

Pain scales are tools used to measure the intensity or quality of pain a patient is experiencing. Commonly used pain scales include:

  • Behavioral Pain Scale (BPS): Often used for non-verbal or critically ill patients, assessing facial expression, upper limb movement, and compliance with ventilation.
  • Numerical Pain Scale: A simple 0-10 scale where 0 represents no pain and 10 represents the worst pain imaginable.
  • Simple Descriptive Pain Scale: Uses descriptive terms like "no pain," "mild pain," "moderate pain," "severe pain," and "very severe pain."
  • Visual Analog Scale (VAS): A straight line where patients mark their pain level, often ranging from "no pain" to "worst pain."
  • Faces Pain Scale: Uses facial expressions to help children or individuals with cognitive impairments rate their pain.

Importance of Pain Scales

Using pain scales helps healthcare providers:

  • Assess pain more objectively.
  • Monitor changes in pain levels over time.
  • Evaluate the effectiveness of interventions.

Revised Trauma Scale (RTS)

The RTS consists of three key categories:

  • Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) (x)
  • Systolic Blood Pressure (SBP) (y)
  • Respiratory Rate (RR) (z)

Scoring

GCS Points SBP Points RR Points
15-13: 4 >89: 4 10-29: 4
12-9: 3 76-89: 3 >29: 3
8-6: 2 50-75: 2 6-9: 2
5-4: 1 1-49: 1 1-5: 1
3: 0 0: 0 0: 0

Interpretation

  • 12: Delayed
  • 11: Urgent
  • 10 or Less: Immediate
