Modes of Ventilation

Controlled Mechanical Ventilation (CMV): Machine controls rate of breathing.
Delivery of preset volume (TV) and rate regardless of patient's breathing pattern. Sedation or paralyzing agent (e.g., Pavulon) usually required.
Assist Controlled Ventilation (ACV): Patient controls rate of breathing. Inspiratory effort triggers delivery of preset volume.
Intermittent Mandatory Ventilation (IMV): Patient breathes spontaneously (own tidal volume) between ventilator breaths of a preset volume and rate.
Synchronized Intermittent Mandatory Ventilation (SIMV): A form of pressure support ventilation.
Administers mandatory ventilator breath at a preset level of positive airway pressure. Monitors negative inspiratory effort and augments patient's spontaneous tidal volume or inspiratory effort.
Synchronized with patient's breathing pattern. Positive End-Expiratory Pressure (PEEP): Increases oxygenation by increasing functional residual capacity (FRC).   • لحماية مجرى الهواء (GCS منخفضة، إفرازات كثيرة...)

الإعدادات الأولية للتهوية

  • Calculate predicted body weight Males: PBW= 50 + 2.3 [height (inches) - 60]
  • Females: PBW= 45.5 + 2.3 [height (inches) -60]
      • Calculate minute ventilation (VE) using body surface area (BSA) Males: BSA x 4 Females: BSA x 3.5
  • وضع التهوية: Assist/Control (Volume هو الشائع)
  • VT = 6 ml/kg PBW
  • RR = VE / VT
  • FiO2: ابدأ بـ 100% ثم خفض تدريجياً حسب الحالة
  • PEEP: ابدأ بـ 5 cmH2O واستخدم جداول ARDSnet
  • I:E Ratio = 1:2 (تعدل حسب الحاجة)

Select Mode

  • Assist/control mode is the preferred ventilator mode for most conditions. The name may vary depending on the type of ventilator (e.g., Draeger- IPPV assist, SCMV, Vol A/C).
  • Decision on whether to put pressure control ventilation (PCV) or volume control ventilation (VCV) depends on the operator and institutional preference. In Nepal. First preferred mode is Volume Assist Control.

Set Tidal Volume (VT)

  • V T is commonly set at 6 ml/kg of predicted body weight (PBW).
  • In PCV, the pressure control should be adjusted to achieve the target V T.

Set Respiratory Rate (RR)

  • Respiratory rate (RR) is set to achieve the target minute ventilation (VE). RR= VE/ V T E.g., if target V E is 6 L and target V T is 300 ml then RR is set at 6000/300= 20 breaths/min.
  • For obstructive airway diseases (e.g., COPD), initial RR should be kept low at 8-12 breaths/min to avoid air trapping and generation of auto PEEP.

Set FiO 2

  • Start with FiO 2 of 100%.
  • بعد استقرار الحالة بنقلل نسبة FiO 2 لتحقيق مستوي الاكسجين المطلوب للحال Targets may vary according to disease condition (e.g., ARDS: PaO2≥55mmHg or SPO2≥ 88%; TBI: SPO2≥ 94%).

Set PEEP

  • Start with a minimum PEEP of 5 cm H2O.
  • Many methods of setting PEEP; most common technique is to use the PEEP- FiO 2 table of ARDSnet trial.
  • Lower PEEP may be required in obstructive airway diseases without spontaneous breathing.
  • PEEP selection can be done from one of the following tables. In Nepal, we commonly use lower PEEP/higher FiO2

Inspiratory time and I:E ratio

  • I:E ratio is set at 1:2.
  • Higher ratio (1:1) may be required for better oxygenation and a lower ratio (1:3) may be required for carbon dioxide removal and for patients with obstructive airway disease (e.g., COPD).
  • I:E ratio can be set directly in some ventilators (e.g., Maquet Servo); in some, this will be determined by setting the inspiratory time and RR (e.g., Drager Evita XL).

PEEP/FiO2 Tables (ARDSnet)

FiO2 PEEP (cmH2O)
0.3 - 0.4 5 - 8
0.5 - 0.6 8 - 10
0.7 - 0.9 12 - 18
1.0 18 - 24

المراقبة أثناء التهوية

  • الضغط: Pplat < 30، و Driving Pressure < 14
  • موجات التهوية: لرصد مقاومة/تسرب/تزامن
  • ثاني أكسيد الكربون: اضبط RR حسب الصيغة، واسمح بفرط الكربون إذا كان pH > 7.2
  • الضغط الدموي: راقب دائماً لاحتمالية حدوث انخفاض بعد بدء التهوية

Weaning Methods

  • IMV/SIMV weaning: Decrease IMV rate every 1-4 hrs. Monitor spontaneous breaths. Obtain ABGs within 30 min of ventilator change. Allows for gradual change from positive-pressure ventilation to spontaneous-pressure ventilation.
  • CPAP/BIPAP: Provides expiratory support, maintains positive intrathoracic pressure. BIPAP adds inspiratory muscle support to CPAP. Prevents respiratory muscle fatigue.
  • PSV: Use low levels of PSV (5-10 cm H₂O). Decrease in 3-6 cm H₂O increments. Useful in retraining respiratory muscles due to long-term ventilation.
  • T-tube weaning: Place patient on T-tube circuit on same FiO₂ as on ventilatory assistance. Monitor ABGs after 30 min. Provide a brief rest period on the ventilator as needed. Continue to monitor ABGs until satisfactory. Extubate when patient is rested, with good spontaneous respiratory effort, and ABGs within acceptable parameters.

Assessing Readiness to Wean

Clinical Criteria

  • Resolution of disease
  • Adequate cough and reduced amount of secretions

Objective Criteria

Adequate Oxygenation

  • SpO₂ ≥ 90% or PaO₂ > 60 mmHg with PEEP ≤ 8 cm H₂O and FiO₂ ≤ 0.4
  • PaO₂/FiO₂ ≥ 150

Adequate Ventilation

  • RR ≤ 35 breaths/min
  • Generated VT > 5 ml/kg
  • Vital capacity > 10 ml/kg
  • No clinically significant respiratory acidosis

Stable Cardiovascular System

  • HR < 140 bpm
  • Stable blood pressure or minimal/no use of pressors or inotropes

Combined Indices

  • Rapid Shallow Breathing Index (RSBI) = RR / VT < 105 (VT in liters)

Adequate Mentation

  • Arousable
  • GCS ≥ 13
  • No continuous sedative infusions

Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT)

Once a patient is assessed as ready to wean, an SBT should be performed daily using any of the following methods:

  • Pressure Support Ventilation (PSV)
  • Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP)
  • T-piece
  • Automatic Tube Compensation (ATC)

Preferred Method

PSV of ≤ 8 cm H₂O for 30 minutes to a maximum of 2 hours (especially in initial weaning trials)

Tolerance Criteria

  • SpO₂ ≥ 90% and/or PaO₂ ≥ 60 mmHg
  • Spontaneous VT ≥ 4 ml/kg PBW
  • RR ≤ 35/min
  • pH ≥ 7.3
  • No signs of respiratory distress (i.e., absence of 2 or more of the following):
    • HR > 120% of baseline
    • Marked use of accessory muscles
    • Abdominal paradox
    • Diaphoresis
    • Marked dyspnea

Nursing assessment during weaning

  • Vital signs and hemodynamics (PAS, PAD, PCWP, CO, CT)
  • Dysrhythmias or ECG changes
  • Oxygenation/Efficiency of gas exchange
  • CO₂ production and elimination
  • pH level
  • Bedside pulmonary function tests
  • Work of breathing indluding use of accessory muscles
  • Level of fatigue
  • Patient discomfort
  • Adequate nutrition

    • If the patient’s SBT is successful, extubation can be performed if there is strong cough.

    • Cuff leak is performed for patients with high risk of subglottic edema, which include
    • Traumatic intubation.
    • Intubation >6 days.
    • Reintubation after unplanned Extubation.
    • Females.
    • Large endotracheal tube for size.

    If extubation has to be postponed, reassessment for possible extubation should be done after 12 hours.

Common Causes of Ventilator Alarms

  • Patient causes:
    • Biting down on endotracheal tube
    • Patient needs suctioning
    • Coughing
    • Gagging on endotracheal tube
    • Patient "bucking" or not synchronous with the ventilator
    • Patient attempting to talk
    • Patient experiences period of apnea
  • Mechanical causes
    • Kinking of ventilator tubing
    • Endotracheal tube cuff may need more air
    • Leak in endotracheal tube cuff
    • Excess water in ventilator tubing
    • Leak or disconnect in the system
    • Air leak from chest tube if present
    • Malfunctioning of oxygen system
    • Loss of power to ventilator
  • Pathophysiological causes
    • Increased lung noncompliance, such as in ARDS
    • Pneumothorax or hemothorax
    • Increased airway resistance, such as in bronchospasm
    • Pulmonary edema
  • Nursing Interventions
    • Check ventilator disconnects and tubing.
    • Insert bite block or oralairway.
    • Check endotracheal cuff pressure.
    • Remove excess water from ventilatortubing
    • Assess breath sounds, suction asneeded.
Problem Symptoms Management
Barotrauma or Volutrauma
Acute lung injury
  • High peak inspiratory pressures
  • Diminished breath sounds
  • Mean airway pressure
  • Tracheal shift
  • Subcutaneous crepitus
  • Hypoxemia
  • Pneumothorax / Tension pneumothorax
  • Pneumomediastinum
  • Pneumoperitoneum
  • Insert chest tube
  • Needle thoracostomy
Intubation of Right Main Bronchus
  • Absent or diminished breath sounds in left lung
  • Unilateral chest excursion
  • Reposition Endotracheal Tube (ETT)
ETT Out of Position / Unplanned Extubation
  • Absent or diminished breath sounds
  • Note tube location at lip (21–22 cm)
  • Reposition ETT or reintubate
  • Restrain only when necessary
Tracheal Damage due to High Cuff Pressure
(>30 cm H₂O)
  • Blood in sputum during suctioning
  • Frequent ventilator alarms
  • Monitor ETT cuff pressure every 4–8 hrs
  • Perform minimal leak technique
  • Ensure minimal occluding volume
Damage to Oral or Nasal Mucosa
  • Skin breakdown or necrosis of lips, nares, or oral mucosa
  • Reposition tube side-to-side of mouth daily
  • Apply petroleum jelly to nares
  • Provide oral care with toothbrush every 2 hrs
Aspiration
  • Feeding visible during suctioning
  • Blue sputum if blue dye is used
  • Possible tracheoesophageal fistulas
  • Use blue dye with enteral feeds if aspiration suspected
  • Keep head of bed elevated 30–45 degrees
  • Administer PPIs or H₂ receptor antagonists
Ventilator-Assisted Pneumonia & Hemodynamics
  • Respiratory infection
  • Increased risk of sinusitis
  • Hypotension
  • Decreased venous return → ↓ cardiac output due to ↑ intrathoracic pressure
  • Decreased CVP, RAP, and preload
  • Refer to section on VAP
  • Assess sputum color and odor
  • Monitor temperature, WBC, ESR
  • Monitor vital signs and hemodynamics
Psychosocial Concerns
  • Fear, anxiety, powerlessness
  • Difficulty sleeping, nightmares
  • Pain, loneliness
  • Administer anxiolytics, sedatives, analgesics
  • Cluster care activities to promote sleep
  • Encourage patient choices when appropriate
  • Allow frequent family visits
  • Keep patient and family informed
Communication Difficulties
  • Impaired ability to communicate
  • Keep communication simple
  • Obtain slate or writing board
  • Use letter/picture chart
  • Use sign language
Urinary Tract Infection
  • Cloudy, concentrated, odorous urine
  • Change or remove Foley catheter
  • Ensure adequate hydration
  • Administer antiinfectives
Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
  • Painful, swollen leg
  • Pain may increase on dorsiflexion
  • Assess for pulmonary embolism
  • Refer to respiratory section if needed
  • Administer heparin or enoxaparin
Paralytic Ileus
  • Absent or diminished bowel sounds
  • Provide nasogastric drainage with intermittent suction
  • Turn and position patient frequently
Inadequate Nutrition / Protein Loss
  • Refer to section on nutrition
  • Start enteral feedings if appropriate
  • Start total parenteral nutrition if GI tract is nonfunctional or contraindicated
Increased Intracranial Pressure
  • Changes in level of consciousness
  • Unable to follow commands
  • Assess neurological status frequently
Fluid Retention
Due to humidification & ADH release
  • Assess for edema
  • Administer diuretics
  • Drain ventilator tubing frequently
Immobility & Skin Breakdown
  • Skin breakdown
  • Turn and position patient frequently
  • Assess skin for breakdown
  • Assist patient out of bed to chair if not contraindicated
  • Keep skin clean and dry
  • Ensure wrinkle-free sheets
Stress Ulcer and GI Bleeding
  • Blood in nasogastric drainage
  • Hematemesis and/or melena
  • Hematest NG drainage, emesis, feces
  • Administer PPIs or H₂ receptor antagonists
